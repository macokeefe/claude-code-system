Executive Summary

Three Waste Sources Found. One Clear Recommendation.

What I found

3 documented waste sources in the assembly stage

Supplier screw errors, out-of-sequence bar attachment, and an inefficient 2-operator weaving model together add avoidable time and labor cost to every chair built.

What it means

The assembly stage alone has 20%+ cycle time reduction potential

A normal assembly run takes ~64 min. When bars arrive out of sequence, it jumps to ~87 min. The supplier screw issue adds 3 min 15 sec to every chair regardless.

Recommendation

Parallelize bars + weaving, and fix the supplier screw spec

Simulation shows this reduces assembly cycle time to ~65 min consistently — a ~25% reduction on delayed chairs and a more predictable, repeatable process.

Supplier Screw Errors — Step 2
Connector Pre-Assembly
✓ Directly observed & timed
Every single sofa built requires rework at Step 2 because the supplier ships wrong screws. Wrong screws on classic connectors (90 sec), wrong screws on directional connectors (90 sec), and wrong orientation on one left connector (15 sec). This is pure waste — zero value added. It happens on 100% of chairs and costs the same every time.

How Much Does the Bar Delay Problem Actually Cost?

Average assembly time vs. how often bars arrive late

One bar delay event has been observed and documented. The true frequency is not yet known. This chart shows what the average cycle time works out to at different delay rates — once the real rate is measured (tracked on the floor over 2–4 weeks), find that number on the x-axis.
How to read this: The red line is today's current state. At any frequency on the x-axis, the gap between the red line and the blue line (Option C) is the per-chair time savings available by implementing the recommendation. The proposed options are flat because they eliminate the bar delay problem entirely — they don't get worse no matter how often delays happen.
